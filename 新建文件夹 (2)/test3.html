Digital Media as Cultural Power

How digitality, convergence, participation, and mediated place reorganize culture, social relations, and political visibility. This site integrates conceptual explanation with grounded examples and visual prompts.

“The medium is the message.”
— Marshall McLuhan
Overview

1. Introduction: Why Treat Digital Media as Cultural Power?

Digital media should not be reduced to neutral channels for entertainment or information delivery. In contemporary societies, digital platforms and networked infrastructures increasingly shape how publics perceive events, how identities are performed and recognized, how political claims gain traction, and how “reality” is evaluated against its mediated representations. In this sense, digital media operate as a distinctive form of cultural power: they structure visibility, attention, memory, and legitimacy.

This site introduces four interrelated analytical lenses:

  • Digitality – media encoded as data, enabling recombination, replication, and personalization at scale.
  • Convergence – the intensifying integration of technologies, industries, and audience practices across media environments.
  • Participation – users functioning as creators, curators, and distributors, not merely consumers.
  • Mediated Place – digital layers that reconfigure how space is experienced, navigated, and made meaningful.

The sections move from technical processes to cultural forms and immersive experiences, before examining how digital mediation transforms the meaning of “place.” Each concept is paired with concrete cases from platforms, popular culture, and digital art in order to connect theoretical claims to observable media practices.

Key claim: Digital media do not simply represent the social world; they participate in producing the conditions under which the social world becomes visible, discussable, and actionable.
Technical Foundations

2. Technical Foundations of Digital Media

2.1 Digitality & Numerical Representation

Digital media are defined by numerical representation: information is encoded in discrete units (binary code), making texts, images, sounds, and videos computationally transformable. As a consequence, a photograph is no longer merely a fixed record of a scene; it becomes a manipulable dataset that can be compressed, filtered, enhanced, or re-generated by algorithmic systems.

Walter Benjamin’s discussion of mechanical reproduction highlights how reproducibility weakens the artwork’s “aura”—its unique presence in time and space. In digital reproduction, this logic becomes more radical: cultural objects may circulate without a stable “original,” as is common with memes, remixes, and AI-generated imagery.

  • Side-by-side, cat.png and cat2.png visualize how digitality converts “capture” into “continuous revision.” In the first, visual qualities appear as empirical features of the scene; in the second, those same qualities are rendered as aesthetic choices, intensifiable through computation.
  • The pair also foregrounds a cultural shift in authenticity: the “realistic” image and the “optimized” image can coexist, yet platforms often normalize enhancement as routine representation. This suggests that digital realism increasingly depends on curated and computed aesthetics.
  • Finally, the comparison illustrates circulation as power: stylized images often align more easily with platform aesthetics, thus traveling further. Digitality therefore reorganizes what becomes visible, valued, and remembered.
  • The contrast clarifies cultural authority: the Mona Lisa’s value is partly produced by provenance (a named author, a historical context, museum validation), whereas an AI image can approximate “classical” style without a stable origin story.
  • This pushes Benjamin’s “aura” problem further: if appearance can be generated cheaply and infinitely, uniqueness cannot be secured by visual form alone. Value becomes contested through context, ownership, and institutional recognition.
  • The pairing also suggests an infrastructural shift in power: cultural legitimacy is increasingly shaped not only by institutions that own originals, but by platforms and model infrastructures that own datasets, generation tools, and distribution channels.

2.2 Networked Media & Many-to-Many Communication

Digital media are networked and decentralized: content production, distribution, and reception occur through interconnected users and platforms rather than a single broadcast center. The transition from one-to-many broadcasting to many-to-many communication enables rapid circulation, collective framing, and emergent publics—while also intensifying contests over truth, attention, and authority.

Network connections are not neutral conduits: they structure attention flows, sustain communities, and enable new forms of power. A rumor in a group chat, a hashtag on X, or a viral TikTok can outpace institutional news cycles—sometimes correcting institutional narratives, sometimes destabilizing them.

Example analysis (expanded):
  • Networked circulation redistributes gatekeeping: visibility can emerge through social forwarding rather than editorial selection. Speed becomes a form of power because it structures what becomes “known” first.
  • Participation is constitutive: sharing and commenting do not merely respond to a clip; they help produce its social meaning and urgency.
  • Institutional statements often arrive after platform circulation, indicating convergence in practice: legacy institutions increasingly respond within an attention environment structured by platforms.
  • The story becomes hypertext-like: the “event” is assembled across linked nodes (capture → post → amplify → respond), not delivered as a single linear narrative.

2.3 Interactivity & User Agency

Interactivity describes the enhanced capacity of users to influence the form, timing, and context of mediated communication. This ranges from clicking and scrolling to co-creating content, voting during live streams, or altering narrative outcomes in interactive formats.

However, interactivity also produces data: participation is frequently captured, quantified, and monetized. Agency and surveillance-like measurement can therefore coexist within the same platform environment.

Example analysis (expanded):
  • Danmu reconfigure viewing into a collective, time-layered encounter: the text is not fixed, but continuously re-authored through audience overlay.
  • Micro-participation accumulates into atmosphere: brief comments collectively produce affective publics (shared anxiety, humor, motivation).
  • The participatory layer is also measurable: timing, density, and engagement patterns become inputs for ranking and recommendation.
  • Interactivity reshapes perception: attention is distributed across multiple voices, producing interpretation that is fragmented yet collectively coordinated.

2.4 Hypertextuality & Nonlinear Navigation

Hypertextuality refers to non-linear textual organization enabled by linking. Readers move laterally across a network of nodes rather than proceeding through a single linear sequence. In digital environments, reading becomes navigation and knowledge becomes pathway-dependent.

Hypertext also blurs the reader/writer boundary: users annotate, tag, remix, and comment, producing distributed meaning-making rather than centralized authorship.

Example analysis (expanded):
  • Hypertextual reading is movement across connected nodes; sense-making emerges through chosen pathways, not only through single texts.
  • Tags appear to expand choice but also classify the world into platform-readable categories, shaping which connections feel legitimate.
  • Recommendation modules reveal power: “what comes next” is partly engineered through ranking systems and platform priorities.
  • Politically, pathway design can broaden perspective or reinforce repetition; hypertext shapes how ideas become connected in public life.

2.5 Automation, Algorithms, and Databases

Digital media systems rely on databases (storage and categorization) and automation (machine-initiated filtering, modification, and customization). Platforms such as TikTok operationalize algorithmic recommendation via big data: cultural visibility is organized through measurable interaction signals rather than solely through human editorial intention.

Algorithms are not neutral: they encode institutional and commercial priorities. They amplify certain voices and suppress others, thereby shaping what becomes “normal,” “trending,” or “important.”

Example analysis (expanded):
  • Automation reduces friction: content ordering and pacing feel natural precisely because decision-making is partially delegated to machines.
  • Algorithms translate behavior into signals (watch time, likes, shares), making “discovery” inseparable from ranking and prediction.
  • Databases make culture comparable: each item is an entry among millions, continuously re-ranked.
  • Personalization can stabilize preferences and narrow exposure to difference, even as it offers convenience.
  • Algorithmic curation functions as cultural governance: it shapes collective visibility and public significance.

3. Protocols, Packets, and Infrastructures: The Material Conditions of Digital Culture

“Code is law.”
— Lawrence Lessig

Discussions of digital culture often foreground platforms, interfaces, and content, yet networked media are fundamentally shaped by less visible layers of infrastructure and protocol. As Lawrence Lessig famously argues, code itself operates as a regulatory force: the technical architecture of networks determines what actions are possible, permissible, or prohibited.

From routing protocols to packet-switching mechanisms, digital communication depends on standardized procedures that coordinate how information is addressed, transmitted, and verified. These infrastructural logics do not merely enable communication; they shape speed, accessibility, surveillance capacity, and control. Cultural power thus operates not only through representation, but through the technical conditions that structure circulation.

3.1 Protocols as Invisible Governance

Internet protocols define how devices identify one another and exchange data. Galloway characterizes protocols as forms of “distributed control,” where power does not reside in a single authority but is embedded within technical standards themselves. Protocols therefore function as a form of governance that is procedural rather than overtly political.

3.2 Packet Switching and Cultural Control

The packet-based architecture of the internet fragments communication into manageable units, allowing data to be rerouted dynamically across networks. While this enhances efficiency and resilience, it also enables monitoring, prioritization, and filtering at granular levels. As a result, cultural flows become technically measurable and governable, linking infrastructure to power.

Theoretical implication: Digital power operates “below the surface,” embedded in protocols and infrastructures that silently organize communication long before content appears on screens.
Convergence Culture

4. Convergence, Participation, and Collective Intelligence

4.1 What Is Convergence Culture?

Convergence is not only a technological trend; it is a cultural transformation in which stories, images, and meanings flow across platforms, and audiences actively follow and assemble them. Old and new media coexist, intersect, and compete within a shared attention economy.

Henry Jenkins argues that convergence occurs both at the level of media infrastructures and in the interpretive labor of audiences who connect dispersed fragments into meaningful narratives.

4.2 Bert–Bin Laden: A Grassroots Icon Meets Global News

A widely cited example involves a manipulated image of Sesame Street’s Bert next to Osama bin Laden. What began as a subcultural joke circulated online and later surfaced in a protest context via professional media reproduction—apparently without comprehension of its origin. The case dramatizes how symbols can travel faster than contexts, producing convergence-driven misunderstanding.

  • In formal reference systems, “典” functions as a stabilized concept supported by institutional authority.
  • In participatory online spaces, meaning becomes situational and relational—shaped by meme logics and interpersonal positioning.
  • As in the Bert case, symbols detach from origin communities and become reattached to new interpretive frames.
  • Convergence reduces contextual control: authority weakens while recognizability increases, producing a tension between stability and mobility.

4.3 Transmedia Storytelling

Transmedia storytelling describes narratives that unfold across platforms, with each medium contributing distinct elements to a larger storyworld. This encourages audience mobility and interpretive labor across films, games, social media, and fan production.

Examples (expanded):
  • The Marvel Cinematic Universe functions as a networked narrative system: films, streaming series, trailers, and fan recaps collectively maintain continuity and expectation.
  • Pokémon exemplifies platform expansion: games, anime, trading cards, AR mobile play, and fandoms deepen the world through medium-specific affordances.
Example analysis (expanded):
  • The MCU operates as accumulation: each release amplifies the next by sustaining attention and extending the storyworld.
  • Convergence is cultural: audiences stitch fragments into coherent narrative wholes through collective interpretation.
  • Transmedia hooks turn spectatorship into ongoing interpretive practice (timelines, crossovers, puzzle-solving).
  • Franchise dominance can set industry expectations and crowd out alternative aesthetics, reshaping cultural visibility.

4.4 Participatory Culture

Participatory culture involves low barriers to creative contribution, informal mentorship, and a sense that contributions matter. Yet participation often occurs within commercial platforms that translate cultural labor into data and profit.

Examples (expanded):
  • Cultural production becomes distributed: making and sharing are normalized social practices rather than professional exceptions.
  • Visibility metrics function as feedback signals that shape creator behavior (titles, thumbnails, pacing, themes).
  • Participation is empowering yet constrained: algorithmic sorting and competitive dynamics shape who gets seen.

4.5 Collective Intelligence

Collective intelligence describes distributed problem-solving through pooled knowledge. Platforms enable collaboration through reply structures, search, quoting, and cross-referencing, yet accuracy depends on norms of verification and correction.

Examples (expanded):
  • Knowledge emerges from many partial inputs; solutions are assembled through revision and accumulation.
  • Quality depends on norms (citation, critique, correction); without them, infrastructure can amplify rumors.
  • Collaboration contains inequality: reputation, moderation, and popularity shape whose knowledge counts.

5. Convergence Beyond Culture: Regulation and Media Industry Power

Convergence is not only a cultural phenomenon; it is also institutional and economic. As media formats become interchangeable (data stored and transferred in compatible digital forms), regulatory frameworks and industry structures are pressured to adapt. This has consequences for competition, innovation, and the diversity of cultural perspectives.

5.1 Technological Convergence: Interchangeability and Manipulability

Convergence implies that media increasingly share formats and infrastructures, becoming interoperable and “networkable.” Digital formats are also compressible, enabling rapid circulation, and manipulable, since content is numerically represented (0/1). These properties intensify circulation and re-editing, but also standardize cultural production around platform-compatible forms.

networkable compressible dense information manipulable (0/1)

5.2 Regulatory Convergence: Shifting Policy in a Digitized Market

Historically, different media industries were regulated through sector-specific regimes (telecom, broadcasting, film). As digitization blurred boundaries, there emerged an urgency to restructure regulatory policies. This shift is often associated with neoliberal policy orientations that reduce cross-market barriers, encourage competition, and position innovation as a market outcome.

A classic historical reference is the regulation of telecom monopolies (e.g., AT&T) and concerns over cross-industry dominance. When regulatory barriers weaken, large firms can expand across markets, reshaping the cultural landscape through infrastructural control.

5.3 Media Industry Convergence: Horizontal and Vertical Integration

Industry convergence includes mergers and acquisitions and integration strategies:

  • Horizontal integration: firms expand across multiple sectors (e.g., tech ecosystems extending into film, cloud, hardware, and mobility).
  • Vertical integration: firms expand across stages of the same production chain, controlling production, distribution, and monetization.

The consequence can be cultural concentration: a small number of giants shape platform infrastructures, distribution channels, and visibility logics. This may reduce innovation and narrow the diversity of values and perspectives that become publicly prominent.

Analytical implication: Convergence should be studied as a cultural-infrastructural assemblage—linking everyday participation to corporate consolidation and regulatory transformation.
Cultural Forms

6. Cultural Forms in the Digital Age

6.1 Aura Loss & Copy Culture

For Benjamin, “aura” refers to an artwork’s unique presence in time and space—its situated existence and distance. Digital media destabilize aura through perfect reproducibility and decontextualized circulation: files can be copied infinitely without degradation, weakening the notion of a singular original.

Copy culture can democratize access but also complicates authorship, ownership, and evidentiary trust. In AI synthesis and deepfakes, realism may be easier to generate than authenticity is to verify.

Example (expanded):
  • Facial modeling translates identity into data points, making expressions transferable and editable across contexts.
  • If mechanical reproduction weakened uniqueness, AI synthesis can undermine authenticity as a stable property of the image.
  • Verification becomes socio-technical: trust shifts from “seeing” to provenance, context, and institutional/collective checks.
  • Aura becomes a negotiated outcome rather than an inherent quality—dependent on context, attribution, and validation practices.

6.2 Rhizomes and Meme Ecologies

Deleuze and Guattari’s “rhizome” metaphor describes non-hierarchical, horizontally spreading structures. Digital culture often appears rhizomatic: memes circulate across communities, mutate, and recombine without a single center. Meaning emerges through repetition-with-difference and platform-driven recombination.

Meme ecosystems such as Doge/Cheems illustrate how shared templates become open cultural resources: no single actor owns the meme, yet participation sustains its evolution.

Examples (expanded):
  • A stable template enables endless micro-variations; variation becomes the dominant creative mode.
  • Rhizomatic growth spreads horizontally without a single authoritative center or endpoint.
  • Memes operate as shared language: legibility and adaptability matter as much as originality.
  • Platform affordances reward modular creativity: small edits produce new iterations that circulate independently.

6.3 Viral Circulation and Platform Logics

Platforms are optimized for virality: algorithmic systems amplify content that triggers rapid engagement and strong affect. Virality can enable grassroots visibility, but it can also intensify misinformation, harassment, and aesthetic standardization.

  • Viral circulation often spreads templates rather than unique originals; recognizability outperforms novelty.
  • Platform genres (night views, check-in angles) function as soft instructions shaping what is rewarded.
  • Viral scripts spill into physical practice: visitors reproduce platform-approved images, turning place into staged performance.
New Part

7. From Consumption to Produsage: Process, Relay, and AI-Augmented Cultural Labor

A distinctive feature of digital culture is the shift from finished products to ongoing processes. Participation frequently takes the form of iterative modification, relay-style remaking, and collaborative accumulation. This can be conceptualized as produsage: users simultaneously produce and use cultural artifacts.

7.1 Relay Videos and Iterative Remix

Relay or “chain” videos illustrate how cultural objects evolve through successive user interventions. Each participant inherits a template and adds a small transformation, producing a cumulative artifact whose authorship is distributed and whose meaning emerges through the sequence rather than a single origin.

  • Process over product: the value lies in participation and iteration, not merely the final clip.
  • Visibility through accumulation: chained replication increases discoverability and platform relevance.
  • Norms and constraints: platform aesthetics and algorithmic incentives shape what counts as “good” remix.

7.2 AI and the Reconfiguration of Creative Agency

AI generation and enhancement tools intensify produsage by lowering the cost of stylistic transformation. Yet this also raises questions about creative agency: if automated systems initiate or strongly guide production, cultural labor may shift from composing forms to selecting prompts, curating outputs, and optimizing for platform circulation.

prompting curation optimization automation
Interpretive takeaway: Produsage does not abolish power; it relocates it—toward platform infrastructures, recommendation systems, and the datasets/models that define what can be generated.
Immersive Experience

8. Immersive Digital Experiences and Hyperreality

8.1 Telepresence: Being There from a Distance

Telepresence describes the mediated sensation of being present across distance. Video conferencing, live streaming, and real-time multiplayer environments enable social coordination in shared time despite spatial separation. Telepresence is strengthened by vividness and interactivity, and it can also produce ambient awareness of distant others (friends not seen for years, online celebrities, and constant social updates).

These systems reshape work, education, and intimacy, while also exposing inequalities in connectivity, devices, and domestic conditions.

  • Telepresence depends on maintaining continuity of perception and turn-taking so that “being together” is experienced as shared time.
  • Participation becomes interface-defined (camera, mute states, visibility), reorganizing what counts as presence.
  • Presence increasingly means integration into a communication system rather than co-location in one physical room.

8.2 Virtual–Actual Hybridity

Contemporary experience often blends virtual and actual layers. “Virtual” can be real yet intangible, while “actual” is real and tangible. Hybrid situations reveal that reality is composed of both. A simple illustration: one might be physically disoriented in a desert, yet locatable through GPS—raising the question of whether one is “lost” or not, and for whom.

Concept analysis :
  • Definition and core idea. Virtual–actual hybridity describes situations where physical presence is continuously shaped by digital information and interfaces, producing mixed environments rather than separate worlds.
  • Example 1: navigation apps. Streets are re-framed by real-time data (traffic, ETA, algorithmic route suggestions), turning cities into interfaces and route choice into platform-guided decision-making.
  • Example 2: AR filters. Filters normalize digitally optimized self-presentation, reshaping norms of authenticity and “presentability.”
  • Power and inequality. Device access and connectivity become forms of social power: the same physical space is not experienced equally without the digital layer.
  • Why this matters. Digital layers do not merely represent reality; they participate in producing what is noticeable, actionable, and valuable.

8.3 Simulation and Hyperreality: When Models Precede the World

Simulation refers to the modeling of reality through representational systems. Hyperreality emerges when representations no longer mirror the world but become the standards through which the world is judged. In digital environments, highly optimized images and cinematic framings can become more influential than the physical situations they depict, leading audiences to prefer copies to originals.

8.3.1 Simulation as Model-Making

Simulations are not “fake” by definition; they are structured models that enable prediction, visualization, and control. Yet as simulations circulate widely, they can become authoritative reference points that reorganize perception and expectation.

Examples (expanded):
  • Processed landscapes redesign nature to match platform aesthetics: smoother water, deeper skies, fewer imperfections. The image becomes a manufactured standard rather than a documentation.
  • Hyperreality emerges when visitors evaluate the physical site against the processed image; representation precedes experience and judges it.
  • When conflict imagery adopts first-person shooter or action-film conventions, violence becomes legible as spectacle and competition rather than human catastrophe.
  • This is politically consequential: hyperreal framing can aestheticize suffering, making violence easier to consume, share, and even admire without confronting consequences.
Mediated Place

9. Digital Media and Mediated Place

9.1 Rethinking “Place” in the Age of Geomedia

Place is not only a geographic coordinate; it is a lived environment shaped by memory, narrative, and social relation. Geomedia (location-aware apps, AR, site-specific digital art) add layers to this process, mediating how places are experienced and interpreted.

When users navigate with smartphones, tag locations, or follow platform-based tours, physical sites and digital representations intertwine. One sees the landscape and the interface at the same time.

9.2 Site-Specific Digital Works

Dead Drops (Aram Bartholl)

Dead Drops embeds USB drives in public walls, enabling offline file exchange. The city becomes a decentralized file-sharing network, extending peer-to-peer logics into physical architecture.

Skies Painted with Unnumbered Sparks (Janet Echelman & Aaron Koblin)

A large suspended sculpture responsive to mobile input: participants generate light patterns, turning the night sky into a collaborative canvas. Place becomes event-like—a convergence of bodies, sensors, and networked signals.

BIG ❤ NYC (Bjarke Ingels Group)

A public installation that visualizes presence and collective affect through responsive illumination, rendering the square as a sensing environment rather than static monument.

Homing (Jenna Burchell)

Visitors touch conductive elements to trigger recorded sounds and stories. Place is produced through tactile interaction—gesture “completes the circuit” and activates narrative memory.

9.3 Place, Power, and Participation

Mediated places are not neutral. Location-aware media can support democratic storytelling by foregrounding local memory and marginalized perspectives, but they can also promote commercial visions that erase conflict and inequality.

Participatory design approaches seek to make geomedia more socially sensitive by treating places as contested and negotiated rather than as empty backdrops for branding and tourism.

Conclusion

10. Conclusion: Digital Media Power as Infrastructure, Culture, and Governance

This site has argued that digital media function as cultural power because they reorganize not only representation but the conditions under which representation becomes visible, shareable, and socially consequential. Digitality enables numerical transformation and infinite reproducibility; networked infrastructures enable many-to-many circulation; interactivity and hypertext produce pathway-based meaning-making; and automation/database logics govern cultural visibility through prediction and ranking.

Convergence, moreover, extends beyond audience practice into regulatory and industrial transformations. As media become interoperable and interchangeable, policy regimes shift and corporate consolidation intensifies, allowing a small number of infrastructures to shape attention economies and cultural diversity. Participation and produsage therefore remain ambivalent: they can enable creativity and collective intelligence, while also being enrolled into commercial data extraction and algorithmic governance.

Finally, immersive experience and mediated place demonstrate that digital media do not merely depict the world; they increasingly shape how the world is experienced. In hyperreality, images become standards for reality; in geomedia, interfaces become part of place. Understanding digital media as cultural power therefore requires analysis across three levels simultaneously: technical infrastructures, cultural forms, and institutional governance.
